Field notes

Six things I keep finding in small-business tenants.

These aren't hypotheticals — they're what I open every invoice and admin center to. If any of them sound like your organization, we should talk. The fix is almost always cheaper and simpler than the sales pitch you've been getting.

01

An E3 tenant with 40 unassigned licenses.

Someone bought Enterprise years ago "to be safe," people left, seats never got reclaimed. I've seen small firms carrying $18,000/year in licenses nobody is logged into.

Common in: 15–75 person firms after a growth spurt or downsize
02

Copilot bought for the whole team.

At $30/user/mo, a 20-person rollout is $7,200/year. Half the seats never write a Word doc. The other half write ten emails a week. Copilot doesn't need to be everywhere. It needs to be somewhere.

Typical waste: 50–60% of licenses under-used in first 6 months
03

The SharePoint site nobody opens.

Twelve nested folders, three orphaned "team" sites, and one Sharon in accounting who knows where the current contract template lives. That's not knowledge management — that's tribal memory with a URL.

Fix time: ~3 weeks for a 30-person firm
04

Business Basic where Business Premium belongs.

Legal, healthcare, financial, HR — organizations touching sensitive client data running on $6/user plans with no Defender, no Intune, no DLP. A single lost laptop is an insurance claim waiting to happen.

Upgrade math: $16/user delta pays for itself on incident one
05

Approvals happening in email.

Expense reports, PTO, new-hire access requests — all threading through five inboxes with no record of who said yes. Power Automate is included in your subscription, and it fixes this in an afternoon.

Tools already owned: Forms + Lists + Flow, no add-on required
06

Copilot with nothing to work with.

Turning on Copilot before you clean up SharePoint is like hiring a research assistant and pointing them at a supply closet. Copilot's answers are only as good as the metadata underneath them. Structure first, agents second.

Order of operations: Content → Structure → Copilot

How I work

Four rules I don't break.

Consulting engagements go sideways when the consultant has more incentive to expand the project than to end it. I've written down the rules I hold myself to — so you can hold me to them too.

01

I don't sell licenses.

Most "Microsoft partners" get a cut when you buy more seats. I don't. That's why the answer is usually fewer licenses, not more — and why the first recommendation on Business Premium is almost always audit what you already have.
02

Copilot goes to the five, not the fifty.

A 20-person firm rarely needs 20 Copilot seats. It needs three to five, aimed at the owner, the analyst, the top salesperson, and whoever spends their day writing. Everyone else gets Copilot Chat (free) and Business Premium. That's the whole strategy.
03

Content structure before AI.

I refuse to configure Copilot on top of a broken SharePoint tenant. It's not fair to the client and it's not fair to the tool. First we fix libraries, metadata, and permissions. Then Copilot Chat becomes an internal expert instead of an expensive keyword search.
04

Every engagement is designed to end.

Your team should be able to run the environment without me. I document what I build, train the people who will maintain it, and prefer a 90-day sprint to a 12-month retainer. If I'm still there in year two, I've done something wrong.
Three quick wins

Improvements I can deliver in a week — using tools already on your invoice.

These are the three most consistently useful things I turn on in a first engagement. Small effort, real time-savings, and nothing on the list requires a new subscription.

i.

Approval workflows that stop living in email.

The expense-report thread with seven replies and no clear "approved" — that's what we fix first. A Forms intake, a SharePoint list to track it, and a Power Automate flow that routes to the manager, logs the decision, and posts to a Teams channel. Done in an afternoon, and it's free with the plans you already own.

ii.

A SharePoint intranet people actually open.

Not a directory of team sites. A curated landing page with news, HR links, the current expense policy, and a search bar that returns the right document first. Once metadata replaces folder-hunting and promoted results replace tribal knowledge, Copilot Chat starts answering real questions like "what's our current PTO policy" with the source cited.

iii.

Copilot, but pointed at the five people it'll change.

Before we deploy a single license, I do a role-by-role review: who writes? who decides? who runs Excel models? Then I put Copilot on those seats — three to five, usually — with prompt playbooks built for their actual work. The rest of the team keeps Copilot Chat (free) and doesn't miss anything.

About the founder

I'm Roger. I teach AI to people who don't work in tech — and I fix the Microsoft tenants they inherit.

I started GrowthPoint Tech AI because I kept meeting the same person: a small business owner or nonprofit leader who was paying Microsoft plenty but couldn't tell you why, running a SharePoint site nobody visited, and being pitched Copilot licenses they didn't need yet.

For years I've been building AI curriculum for real-world professionals — realtors, insurance agents, HR leads, small-firm owners — the people Microsoft's product marketing doesn't quite reach. That work taught me one thing very clearly: the gap isn't the technology. Microsoft has built astonishing tools. The gap is the distance between a busy professional and the confidence to use them well.

This practice exists in that gap. I run license audits, design SharePoint architectures that don't collapse under their own folder tree, roll out Copilot to the roles that will actually use it, and train the humans left holding the keyboard afterward. Use what you already pay for first — new licenses only when they've earned their place.

I'm a one-person practice on purpose. That means you talk to the person doing the work. It also means I take on a small number of engagements at a time — usually SMB, nonprofits, and independent consultancies between 5 and 75 people.
